# The Penance

A few of my friends and my mother know that as soon as I returned from the UK in Autumn 2024, I started a part-time job. I work as a teacher for an institution that specialises in academic tutoring for Chinese students studying abroad. In case you don’t know, this is a grey-industry in fact as all schools assume you are completing your assessment independently or with mostly-controlled assistance. So working in this industry often involves practices that can regarded as academic misconduct.


To be honest, It is not a well-paid job. They charge students on a hourly rate basis, usually around CN¥800-1200 per hour (as you get discount if you purchase by bulk) and I receive less than a quarter of that. Not to mention that 1 hour of online teaching requires hours of preparation that can easily keep your ass glued on a chair from afternoon to evening. Still, it covers some of my expenses at least, considering I only earn a marginal monthly income for someone living in Beijing. But that’s a story for another time.

In my mother’s word, only kids who can’t make it academically would purchase these services, proved she’s right. Their customers are often students with intermediate or limit academic literacies. If you’re lucky, your student might produce a pretty decent, or at least adequate, essay after a one-hour lecture on an outline you built for them. In those cases, it only takes a few minutes to identify minor issues afterwards. Most of the time, however, they simply hand in craps, and it takes a massive effort just to teach them how to properly cite a theorist in Harvard format.

But the most exhausting, nightmarish part is those who purchased "essay polishing" services. This requires you to proofread and modify a student’s work to ensure it won't be flagged by Turnitin’s plagiarism and AI detection services.

# Man vs. Machine

The Turnitin AI detector rates AI suspicion on a percentage system; anything below a certain threshold will marked as a “%”, and that is usually 20%. However, the biggest problem with AI detectors is that their results, to be straightforward, are a hit-or-miss. The detection algorithm is essentially a black box; no one, not even Jesus, could possibly predict what happens once you submit an article to their systems.

My recent experience with these detectors has been tiring and confusing. I once took an order from a student at an Australian university where the policy forced her to take an elective course with zero association with her degree program. After I rewrote the majority of her essay and submitted the document to Turnitin, the result came back as 26% AI. Since this was above the threshold, I had to rework to “humanise" the writing. By simply changing a few words to more colloquial ones, it passed effortlessly.

I wasn’t so lucky the next time. A student from a Russell Group university in Northern England (just one hour away from my alma matar), who was taking a course on the music industry, was struggling to produce a blog article. She had zero knowledge of Western music or industrial dynamics. When I asked why she picked the course, she simply said she "liked music," forgetting that she hadn’t even listened to a Taylor Swift track (or maybe she knew "Love Story" at most).

My lecture with her was an absolute nightmare. I spent over 40 minutes just explaining how music copyrights and royalties work. But that was only the beginning. When I asked her to explain "political economy" in her own words, she answered: “Political economy is political economy.” WOW, such an insightful argument. The lecture, which was supposed to take an hour, stretched to almost 80 minutes, and that was only because the system cuts you off after a 20-minute overrun.

A few days later, I received her first draft of the 1,000-word blog. It turned out she hadn't understood even a quarter of what I taught. It looked nothing like an article you’d find on The Conversation. The structure and rationale were more chaotic than a ball of wool that your cat is playing when no one’s around; the assignment was hopeless, and I had to rewrite every single sentence for her.

After spending an entire afternoon and the following morning reworking her gibberish, the AI score increased from 80% to a whopping 100%. I confess I asked Gemini to help improve the grammar, but seriously? 100%? It eventually took five gruelling revisions to finally get that “Unlikely AI” rating on Turnitin.

I have showed Turnitin every possible trick that was worth it: switching to passive voice, breaking long sentences into short ones (even when it made zero sense and disrupted the narrative), replacing formal language with Reddit-style slang, or moving sentences to different paragraphs. It was far worse than editing product FAQs for a smartphone manufacturer back when I worked at a PR agency—at least they were comfortable with AI.

# The Rationale

Reading about my sense of inadequacy and perplexity, you might resonate with me or think the institution was pushing me too hard. But I am just a very small, neglectable molecule in this higher education system. What I want to say is that the implementation of AI detection services is catastrophic. In my opinion, they should never have been invented since day 1. 

To begin with, I think it is important to show you how AI detectors respond to written content. While these are not Turnitin scores, the underlying algorithms across these platforms is very similar. Here is how they "scored" selected media:

- An essay I wrote before I realised you could ask GPT to polish your writing (saving me from clicking “accept” a thousand times on Grammarly): 20% AI
- Several paragraphs from a book written by my professor before LLMs even existed: 23% AI
- A section from my own dissertation, which I asked ChatGPT to improve for flow, clarity, and grammar: 26% AI
- Apple’s press release for the MacBook Pro (M5 Pro/Max chips): 51% AI
- Taylor Swift’s Midnights Album Prologue: 55% AI

The case of Taylor Swift is very telling. The Midnights prologue was written in early 2022 at the latest, as it generally takes six months or more for vinyl factories to press and package records. This was way before the world was shocked by GPT-3.5 in late 2022. So, there is no possible way she could asked an AI to write her prologue—unless someone at OpenAI infiltrated her management team to leak a copy of the record illegally.

Taylor’s writing revealed how AI detectors function: they are not "virtual police dogs" capable of sniffing out AI text. A more suitable description is that they operate more like primary school students comparing pre-determined patterns of "AI-generated" versus "human" content, looking for subtle patterns and deviations that don't actually prove authorship.

# The rest is under construction

The emergence, and proliferation of AI detection services is essentially a panic response from higher education and their associated industrial organisations. After the disruption of large language models, institutions are desperately looking for a sense of 'certainty' in a fearsome and wretched landscape.

The implementation of AI detection services has even gave birth to something no one could have predicted: we have written manuals teaching you how to prompt AI chatbots to “humanise” your writing. These curated guides have word-by-word prompt samples that you can simply copy-paste into a chat box.

Asking an AI to produce something that is considered "more human" than an actual human being—a highly sophisticated creature that requires carbohydrates, protein, and fat to survive—truly illustrates our post-modern reality, one in which "realness" has dissolved. When AI-modified text appears more natural than a student’s own words, we have to ask ourselves: has AI become so "humane" that it is now a simulacrum of a human being?

If AI-generated text can trick another AI whose job is to identify AI, then how do we define the fundamental characteristics of human literature? We are trapped in a loop where the machine is the judge, the jury, and the witness of our own humanity.
